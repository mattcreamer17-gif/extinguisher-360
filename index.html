The product works. The question is what it becomes.

Extinguisher 360 turned NFPA 10 monthly inspections into a $1.49-per-unit-per-month app, locked in a reference like Sazón (Goya Foods DR), and collected pitch wins at Harvard Angels, AMCHAM DR, and Volcano Summit. The next question is the one that decides whether this becomes a fire extinguisher app or a facilities compliance platform. When you add emergency lights, exit signs, AED units, and fire doors, where does the rule engine live, where does the per-jurisdiction override live, and where does the customer-specific template live.

How we would frame your stack.

60%Traditional

Database and form work.

Assets, inspections, timestamps, photo storage, reporting. This is Postgres territory. Fast, cheap, boring, reliable. This is most of what you already built.

30%Rule-based

Checklists and compliance logic.

NFPA 10 today. NFPA 25 for sprinklers next. Local codes in each LATAM market. Insurance-carrier-specific checklists for any channel partnership. Deterministic logic. Not a job for an LLM.

10%Real AI

Where AI earns its keep.

Photo-based damage detection. Anomaly flagging across inspection history. A template-generation layer that lets a partner stand up a new asset type without your engineers in the loop.

If those three layers are cleanly separated, the product scales to multi-asset without fragmenting. If they are tangled, every new asset type is a fresh engineering sprint, and the burn rate spikes right as you go to raise.

We have run this pattern before.

VigilOre, a compliance platform in Democratic Republic of Congo mining. A different vertical. Same shape of problem: rules heavy, document heavy, jurisdiction heavy. The engagement compressed 160 hours of compliance work into under five minutes of orchestrated output. Read that as "what the multi-asset version of your product looks like on a hard day."

The methodology behind the layer separation is Interpretable Context Methodology (ICM), a folder-structure-as-agent-architecture framework submitted to ACM TiiS. Open source under MIT. It is useful here because the product architecture question you are going to face in 12 months is the one ICM was written to answer. Read the paper on GitHub.

A few discovery questions we would bring.

  1. When a customer asks for emergency-light inspections alongside extinguishers, how long does it take to stand up a new checklist template, and who owns that inside a seven-person team.
  2. For LATAM expansion, where does the per-country compliance rule live today. In the app, in a database config, or still in the founders' heads.
  3. When you go out to raise, is the story "fire extinguisher SaaS" or "facilities compliance platform," and what has to be true in 12 months to make the second story defensible.
  4. Who owns the insurance-carrier channel conversation, and how far along is it.
